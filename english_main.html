Where the mind is without fear..

Poem

Where the mind is without fear and the head is held high; Where knowledge is free; Where the world has not been broken up into fragments By narrow domestic walls; Where words come out from the depth of truth; Where tireless striving stretches its arms towards perfection; Where the clear stream of reason has not lost its way Into the dreary desert sand of dead habit; Where the mind is led forward by thee Into ever-widening thought and action— Into that heaven of freedom, my Father, let my country awake. -Rabindranath Tagore

Summary

In this poem, Tagore expresses his vision for a free and enlightened India.He dreams of a nation where people can live fearlessly, with dignity, and where knowledge is accessible to all.The poet also calls for unity, truth, and reason, rejecting divisive barriers like caste, religion, and regionalism. The poem is a plea for progress, not only politically, but also intellectually and morally, as the poet appeals to a higher power to guide his country to a better, more liberated future. The themes of the poem are deeply rooted in freedom, truth, and the pursuit of knowledge, all of which are critical for the betterment of a nation and its people.

Figure of Speech

1. Where knowledge is free;” The figure of speech in this line is Metaphor. Explanation: The phrase "knowledge is free" is a metaphor that compares knowledge to a resource available to everyone. It suggests a society where education and wisdom are accessible without restrictions. 2. “Where tireless striving stretches its arms towards perfection;” The figure of speech in this line is Personification. Explanation: The act of "striving" is personified as something that can "stretch its arms." This gives human qualities to the abstract concept of striving, showing it as reaching out for perfection. 3. “Where the mind is without fear and the head is held high;” The figure of speech in this line is Alliteration. Explanation: The repetition of the "h" sound in "head" and "held high" creates a rhythm. This emphasizes the idea of dignity and self-respect. 4. “Where the clear stream of reason has not lost its way, into the dreary desert sand of dead habit;” The figure of speech in this line is Repetition. Explanation: The word "where" is repeated at the beginning of several lines, which helps to create a rhythmic structure and emphasizes the poet’s vision of an ideal world. 5. “Where words come out from the depth of truth;” The figure of speech in this line is Hyperbole. Explanation: The phrase "depth of truth" is an exaggeration. It emphasizes that words should come from the most sincere, honest place, stressing the importance of truth in communication

Meaning

1. Striving:Making great efforts to achieve or obtain something. 2. Fragments: or broken pieces of something. 3. Dreary:,bleak and lifeless 4. Dead habit: customs or practices that no longer have meaning or value. 5. Thee:An old English word meaning "you," often used in prayer or addressing God. 6. Ever-widening: expanding or growing broader.

Poem

All the world's a stage, And all the men and women merely players; They have their exits and their entrances; And one man in his time plays many parts, His acts being seven ages. At first, the infant, Mewling and puking in the nurse's arms. And then the whining schoolboy, with his satchel And shining morning face, creeping like snail Unwillingly to school. And then the lover, Sighing like furnace, with a woeful ballad Made to his mistress' eyebrow. Then a soldier, Full of strange oaths, and bearded like the pard, Jealous in honour, sudden and quick in quarrel, Seeking the bubble reputation Even in the cannon's mouth. And then the justice, In fair round belly with good capon lined, With eyes severe and beard of formal cut, Full of wise saws and modern instances; And so he plays his part. The sixth age shifts Into the lean and slippered pantaloon, With spectacles on nose and pouch on side; His youthful hose, well saved, a world too wide For his shrunk shank, and his big manly voice, Turning again toward childish treble, pipes And whistles in his sound. Last scene of all, That ends this strange eventful history, Is second childishness and mere oblivion, Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything. -William Shakespeare

Summary

Shakespeare's "All the world's a stage" compares life to a play in which people are actors, passing through seven stages of life. These stages ar 1. Infancy - Helpless, crying in the nurse’s arms. 2. Childhood - A reluctant schoolboy, dragging himself to school. 3. Young love - A passionate, sighing lover writing poems for his beloved. 4. Soldier - A brash, honor-seeking soldier, eager for fame and quick to quarrel. 5. Middle age - A wise judge, with a formal appearance, offering advice. 6. Old age - A frail, thin old man, physically declining and becoming childlike. 7. Oblivion - The final stage, where a person loses all faculties and fades into nothingness. The speech reflects the inevitability of aging and the transient nature of life.

Figure of Speech

1. “And all the men and women merely players;" The figure of speech in this line is Metaphor. Explanation: The term "players" metaphorically refers to people, indicating that they are acting out their lives in various roles. 2. “At first, the infant, mewling and puking in the nurse's arms.”............. The figure of speech in this line is Imagery. Explanation: The vivid description of the infant creates a strong visual image, evoking the helplessness and vulnerability of early life. 3. “Sighing like furnace, with a woeful ballad made to his mistress' eyebrow."............. The figure of speech in this line is Simile. Explanation: TThe comparison of the lover's sighing to that of a "furnace" illustrates intense emotion, highlighting the passion and longing felt in love. 4. “Full of strange oaths, and bearded like the pard,” The figure of speech in this line is Simile.............. Explanation: The comparison of the soldier to a "pard" (leopard) emphasizes his fierce and bold nature 5. “Sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything.”............. The figure of speech in this line is Repetition. Explanation: The repeated use of "sans" (meaning "without") emphasizes the loss and emptiness experienced in old age, culminating in the complete absence of faculties.

Meaning

1. Stage:A raised platform for performances; metaphorically, the setting for life. 2. Players:Actors in a play; in this context, people living their lives. 3. Mewling:Crying feebly; often associated with infants. 4. Satchel:A bag used for carrying books; symbolizes a schoolboy's journey. 5. Bubble reputation: A fleeting or superficial sense of honor; highlights the pursuit of fame. 6. Pantaloon:An old man; signifies aging and decline. 7. Bubble reputation: A fleeting or superficial sense of honor; highlights the pursuit of fame. 8. Oblivion:State of being forgotten or unknown; signifies the end of life.
